Preliminaries and plumbing:

The data collection is done by the included scripts. Not included is a file "SF.ini" with a section "[SF]" which has the key 'apikey' with a value set to (surprise!) the api key you want to use. - gmstub.py starts the level and saves the account and venue info to a config file - l6_dump_accts.py begins to trawl through orders, using the information leak in the 'cancel orders' API endpoint, to discover accounts trading on the venue; it then uses subprocesses to spin off instances of l6_snoop when it discovers a new and "active" account. - Each instance of l6_snoop.py uses the no-authentication-required websocket interface to grab all the executions on the discovered account, and throws them to a ZMQ intra-process queue. - l6_mon.py reads everything off from the queue and prints it to stdout - This notebook analyzes the data

I usually run it in two separate windows.

In the first one:

python gmstub.py start making_amends --save 

python l6_dump_accts.py

And in the second:

stdbuf -o0 python l6_mon.py |tee rawdata.txt

Other notes: - All of this is done in an Ubuntu 14.04 VM with a more-or-less current Anaconda installation + libraries.
- l6_snoop.py started life somewhere around level 3 as the websocket ping client example code from Twisted, and was modified from there. A brief inspection will no doubt show I know just enough about Twisted to be dangerous. - Honesty compels me to admit that this is the highlight reel, and doesn't show the many and varied dead ends I went down trying to figure out how to find the anomalous account. Was overthinking it quite a bit (a wavelet transform actually got me to the answer, but the regulator-friendly version of the evidence that doesn't involve Hilbert spaces is the one I went with).

To start with, we look at some aggregate statistics of the data. There's generally three very large (institutional?) investors who usually take a beating, but reliably have a mean order size at least 10x the volume of everyone else. We are going to assume that our insider trader can't move that kind of volume.

If we sort by maximum revenue on a single order, and ignore the high-volume institutional traders, then we can usually see that one trader bubbles to the top. It might take a bit as some orders may fill over time, but by trading day 200 or so it's usually a dead ringer. The same trader also generally has a relatively low order count, but a slightly higher-than-normal mean "original quantity" per order. This is what you might expect from someone who's timing the market with inside information. Their single largest sale is also much larger sales (in terms of dollar amount) than any of the other traders, closer to the scale of an institutional trader than a market maker.

Sorry about the legends. But they don't obscure the main point: we can split the active non-institutional accounts into two groups: - Market makers: - position versus time has a sawtooth pattern; they're constantly moving shares - the absolute dollar amount of any given transaction is relatively small - Our suspect: - Has a position versus time trajectory with a much lower frequency pattern; they will go through long periods where they really only fill orders in one direction - Has a handful of orders with extremely large dollar amounts

It's important to note that we're missing trades at the beginning, so the absolute value of the position is unreliable; if we missed a lot of buys at the beginning, then we'll think they're going short when they're really just closing a position, and similarly for sells, but the relative change in position is informative. While the market makers all have a position over time with a sawtooth pattern, the suspect account appears comfortable with holding a steady position for long periods of time inbetween dropping large orders into the market.

While it's hard to say whether our suspect is shorting then covering, or building a position then selling off, they are definitely making extremely large moves with a regular pattern.
